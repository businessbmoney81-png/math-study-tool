Mean & Median
The two measures of center. Always the first step in any descriptive stats problem.
Mean is the average — add everything up, divide by how many you have. It's where the data balances.

Median is the middle value when sorted. It's more resistant to outliers than the mean — one extreme value can pull the mean far off center but barely moves the median.
Example vs Rule

→Scores: 68, 72, 85, 90, 95

2n = 5
3Mean = 410 ÷ 5 = 82
→Median: Sort → 68, 72, 85, 90, 95
Middle value (3rd of 5) = 85
Note: mean (82) ≠ median (85) because 68 pulls the mean down slightly. Outliers always affect the mean more than the median.

Standard Deviation
How spread out your data is from the mean. The foundation of everything else.
Imagine everyone in Vacaville died at exactly age 77. The SD would be zero — no spread at all. But ages ranged from 61 to 86, so there's variation, and SD captures that.

Small SD = data tightly clustered. Large SD = data widely spread.

The formula squares every deviation so negatives cancel, averages them (variance), then square-roots back to original units.
Example vs Rule
📝 General Example — 5 Test Scores
→Scores: 68, 72, 85, 90, 95  |  Mean = 82
1Subtract mean, square each:
(68−82)² = 196
(72−82)² = 100
(85−82)² = 9
(90−82)² = 64
(95−82)² = 169
2Sum = 538
3÷ (n−1) = 538 ÷ 4 = 134.5 ← Variance
4√134.5 = SD ≈ 11.6
Meaning: scores typically vary by about 11.6 points from the mean of 82.
Standard Deviation (Sample)
⚠️
Professor writes SD instead of σ (sigma). Same thing — SD is just plainer notation. In higher-level courses you'll see σ exclusively.
SD = √ Σ (x − x)² n − 1
x : each individual data value
x : sample mean (x-bar)
n−1 : Bessel's correction — always for sample data
Variance (step before SD)
Variance = Σ (x − x)² n − 1
SD = √Variance
Binomial SD (shortcut)
⚠️
In binomial problems the professor uses σ (sigma) for SD. Same concept, just the distribution provides a shortcut so you don't need the long method.
σ = √ ( n · p · q )
n : number of trials
p : probability of success
q : probability of failure (= 1 − p)

Why n−1 and not n?
Dividing by n underestimates the true population spread. Using n−1 corrects this bias. This is called Bessel's correction. In your first practice problem you divided by n=20 and got SD=2.10, but the correct answer using n−1=19 was 2.154. Same math, different denominator — and it changes your final answer.
Key Rules

Coined by Karl Pearson in 1894. "Standard" meaning a reference unit, "deviation" from Latin deviare (to turn from the way). The σ symbol was chosen as the Greek S — for Sum, since SD involves summing squared differences.

Z Score & Z Table
How many standard deviations a value is from the mean — and what percentage of the population falls below it.
The Z score answers: "How unusual is this value?" A Z of 0 means exactly average. A Z of +2 means 2 SDs above average (unusual). A Z of −1.5 means 1.5 SDs below average.

The Z score tells you how unusual a value is. The Z table then converts that Z score into a percentage — telling you what fraction of the population falls below that value.

Min/Max Boundaries & Outliers
The mathematical fence that identifies values that don't belong.
The min/max formula creates a fence around your data. Any value outside that fence is flagged as an outlier — not by intuition, but by math. About 95% of normal data falls within 2 standard deviations of the mean.
Example vs Rule

→Max = 76.8 + 2(5.99) = 88.78
→61 < 64.82 ✗ → OUTLIER
→Remove 61, recalculate everything:
New mean=77.93, New SD=3.075
New Min=71.78, New Max=84.08
SD dropped from 5.99 → 3.075. One outlier nearly doubled the spread.

Min =x − 2 · SD
Max =x + 2 · SD

Max =μ + 2σ
same logic, different symbols
If value is outside range:
→ It's an outlier → Remove it → Recalculate EVERYTHING: Mean, Median, SD, Min, Max, Z scores

📖 Etymology
t-Distribution
Developed by William Sealy Gosset in 1908, published under the pseudonym "Student" (his employer Guinness didn't want competitors to know they used statistics). That's why it's called the Student's t-distribution. He developed it specifically for small brewery samples.
Alpha (α)
From the Greek alphabet — the first letter, used to denote the primary significance threshold. In hypothesis testing, α is the probability of wrongly rejecting a true null hypothesis (Type I error).

📖 Etymology
Factorial (n!)
The ! notation was introduced by Christian Kramp in 1808 as a convenient shorthand. Before that, mathematicians wrote out "n(n-1)(n-2)..." in full. The word "factorial" comes from Latin factorius (of making). 0!=1 is defined this way because it makes combination formulas work correctly when choosing 0 items.
Bessel's Correction (n−1)
Named after astronomer Friedrich Bessel (1784–1846), though he didn't invent it. The correction accounts for the fact that a sample's deviations are measured from the SAMPLE mean (not the true population mean), which introduces a downward bias that n−1 corrects.
